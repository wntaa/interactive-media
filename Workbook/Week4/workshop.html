Workshop Lecture

Introduction to Generative Art

The workshop introduced the concept of generative art, exploring its historical development and influential practitioners. A key question addressed was when generative art began, with connections made to early Dada experiments with chance and randomness. The lecture highlighted several pioneering artists whose work exemplifies different approaches to algorithm-driven creativity.

Notable Generative Artists

  • Yoko Ono

    Though not typically categorized as a generative artist in the computational sense, Ono's instructional pieces and event scores from the 1960s onward represent important conceptual predecessors to algorithmic art, establishing rule-based frameworks for artistic creation.

  • Harold Cohen

    Creator of AARON, one of the earliest and most sophisticated AI art-making programs. Beginning in 1973, Cohen developed this system that could autonomously create original drawings and paintings, raising questions about creativity and authorship in computational art.

  • Manfred Mohr

    A pioneer of algorithmic art who began creating computer-generated art in 1969. His work explores the logical structure of geometric forms, often using algorithms to generate visual complexity from simple mathematical rules.

  • Samuel Monnier

    Contemporary digital artist working with generative systems, particularly known for creating complex fractal-based imagery and patterns using custom algorithms and mathematical formulas.

  • Angela Bulloch

    Contemporary artist whose "pixel boxes" and rule-based installations explore the intersection of digital systems, algorithmic patterns, and physical space.

Research Notes

Further research is needed to explore the connections between early Dadaist experiments with chance operations and contemporary generative art practices. Additional investigation into machine-based mark-making techniques and their historical development would provide valuable context for understanding the evolution of generative art methodologies.

Reflection

Connections Between Kusama and Generative Art

Yayoi Kusama's work, while not explicitly generative in the computational sense, shares interesting parallels with generative art principles. Her use of repetition, pattern, and rule-based creation (particularly in her "Infinity Rooms" and dot-based works) demonstrates how algorithmic thinking can exist in analog contexts. The participatory nature of her installations, where visitors become part of the artwork and sometimes contribute to it, reflects generative art's interest in systems that evolve through interaction.

The exhibition visit and lecture together provided a multifaceted perspective on how artists can create frameworks or systems that generate complex visual experiences. Whether through Kusama's immersive environments or through the computational systems of artists like Harold Cohen, both approaches explore how simple rules and iterations can produce rich, emergent aesthetic outcomes.
